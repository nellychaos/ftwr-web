Most of sports analytics is for sale. Picks, courses, VIP channels, premium tiers. The incentives are clear: tell people what they want to hear, gate the useful stuff, optimise for subscriber retention over honest analysis.

FTWR exists as the opposite shape. It is a commons.

## What FTWR is

FTWR is a community-stewarded commons for sports data modelling. It curates tools, convenes discussion, and documents shared methods as public goods -- non-rival, non-excludable, MIT-licensed, anonymously stewarded. A map and a meeting hall, not a lab or a persona.

The premise is simple. Modelling knowledge is fragmented across private Discords, dead blogs, paywalled courses, and a handful of academic papers that nobody implements. Good tools are scattered across GitHub with no index. A new modeller spends weeks finding what a community with shared memory could surface in minutes. FTWR is the shared memory.

## The six artifacts

FTWR ships six things. None are proprietary. All are owned by the commons.

- **The Map** (`ftwr-map`) -- a curated, PR-driven directory of tools, datasets, papers, and models. Every entry has a category, a neutral description, a contributor handle, a last-verified date, and a link to the canonical source. If a new modeller ever asks "where do I start?", this is the first answer.
- **The Starters** (`ftwr-starters`) -- minimal runnable reference implementations of canonical methods. Poisson goal models in fifty lines. Elo in fifty lines. Closing-line value, Kelly sizing, bootstrap backtests. Each one points at the canonical academic or industry implementation it is teaching. These are educational scaffolds, not proprietary tools.
- **The RFCs** (`ftwr-rfcs`) -- numbered, dated methodology proposals. Backtest protocols, data quality standards, model evaluation criteria. Drafted by any contributor, discussed on the forum, merged when rough consensus emerges. Over time, a citable body of shared standards.
- **The Archive** (this site) -- explainers, method walkthroughs, RFC digests, and guest posts credited by handle. The long-form record.
- **The Forum** (`community.ftwr.app`) -- the meeting hall. Questions get answered. RFCs get debated. New contributors meet reviewers. Tier 1, not a satellite.
- **The Ledger** -- every dollar that comes into FTWR through Open Collective and every dollar that goes out is published. Transparency instead of trust.

## Why a commons

Individual edges erode. A model built alone hits a ceiling set by one person's time and blind spots. But the ability to *find* edges compounds when knowledge is pooled. Better indexes surface better tools. Better tools lower the activation energy for new modellers. More modellers produce more analysis, more critique, more methods -- which improves the index again.

The loop only works if every layer stays open. The moment any piece becomes proprietary -- a paywalled tutorial, a gated scanner, a "VIP" forum -- the compounding stops and the incentives invert. Contributors stop contributing when someone else is capturing the value. Readers become customers. The commons becomes a funnel.

FTWR is built to avoid that failure mode by design. No paid tiers. No courses. No affiliate links. No sponsored entries in The Map. No consulting funnel. Infrastructure costs are funded transparently through Open Collective with a public ledger. The work itself stays free, forever.

## What FTWR is not

- Not a tips service. No picks, no wagers, no "the model says bet this." The archive and forum discuss methods and market structure, not specific bets.
- Not a proprietary toolmaker. FTWR does not compete with the tools in The Map. It indexes and teaches; it does not ship original models under its own name.
- Not a persona. There is no founder to follow. Follow the work.
- Not a business. No revenue, no growth targets, no fundraising. Success is measured in contributors, not users.
- Not a Discord. Asynchronous, indexed, archival.

## Anonymity and stewardship

FTWR is anonymously stewarded as a matter of design, not convenience. A commons does not need a face. Wikipedia does not have a founder's headshot on the homepage.

Maintainers operate under rotating handles. Each handle has a forum profile and a GitHub identity; none are tied to real names, and all can be retired and replaced. The current steward pool is published. New maintainers are proposed via RFC and added by rough consensus. Decisions that affect the commons -- adding categories, changing curation policy, accepting or refusing funding -- happen in public. No backchannels.

Anonymity without documented process is opaque. Anonymity with documented process is institutional. FTWR is the second thing.

## How to contribute

- **Add to The Map.** Found a tool, dataset, or paper worth indexing? Open a PR against `ftwr-map` with the entry template. Every entry credits its contributor.
- **Ship a Starter.** If a canonical method is missing a minimal reference implementation, propose one. Fifty lines that runs on sample data beats five hundred that runs on a private dataset.
- **Draft an RFC.** Got a view on how backtests should be conducted, or what counts as a fair model comparison? Draft it. The process is the product.
- **Answer on the Forum.** The single highest-leverage contribution is a good answer to a stranger's real question. Forum threads compound in search. A good answer becomes public infrastructure.
- **Write for the Archive.** Guest posts are welcomed and credited by handle. Tool comparisons, method walkthroughs, market analysis -- if it teaches, it belongs.

## What comes next

The commons is early. The Map has its first entries. The forum has its first threads. RFC-0001, which codifies the RFC process itself, is the first piece of self-documenting governance.

The single best signal at any point: has someone nobody has met contributed to the commons? If yes, it is real.

How deep does the rabbit hole go? Only one way to find out -- together.
